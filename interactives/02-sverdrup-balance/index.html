Sverdrup balance

Wind blowing over the ocean produces a curl. The curl, divided by β (planetary vorticity gradient), gives the meridional transport. Integrate that, and you get the gyres on a map. This is the simplest equation that explains why the Atlantic and Pacific have the gyres they do.

positive curl (cyclonic forcing — drives subpolar gyre) negative curl (anticyclonic — drives subtropical gyre)
Curl pattern: double gyre

The math

The depth-integrated meridional transport V(x,y) obeys, in steady state:

  β · V = curl(τ) / ρ₀

With V = ∂ψ/∂x (transport-streamfunction definition), integrating eastward from the eastern boundary x_E where ψ(x_E, y) = 0:

  ψ(x, y) = (1/β) ∫_x^{x_E} curl(τ) dx'

That's it. Stommel (1948) and Sverdrup (1947) showed this single equation predicts where the major ocean gyres sit and which way they spin. What it does not tell you: how fast the western boundary current is. For that you need viscosity (Munk 1950) or friction (Stommel 1948) — see the next interactive.

What to play with

Drag β. Higher β (faster planetary rotation gradient) means a given wind makes a weaker gyre. Realistic Earth values are roughly β ≈ 2 in our nondimensional units.

Switch curl pattern. "Double gyre" is the textbook setup — subtropical and subpolar gyres of opposite sign. "Realistic-ish" splits curl into three meridional bands closer to what ERA5 actually measures (positive at high lat, negative in subtropics, positive again at the equator). The streamfunction adapts.

Try β = 0.5. Watch the gyres get massive. This is what tropical-ocean dynamics would look like on a slowly-rotating Earth — much larger circulations from the same wind.

Where this fits

This is the steady-state limit of the v1a barotropic vorticity equation running in amoc-jax. There, instead of integrating the steady balance directly, we integrate the time-dependent vorticity equation forward and watch it relax to this Sverdrup answer (plus a thin western boundary layer that this interactive doesn't capture).

The cos(φ) bug we found and documented in physics.md §2 would have suppressed exactly this β·V balance at high latitudes — when the simulator was wrong, gyres didn't form because the term that creates them was being killed. Fixing it is what made the gyres in the full simulator finally appear.
