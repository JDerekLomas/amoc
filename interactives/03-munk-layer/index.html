Munk boundary layer

In a closed basin, the interior Sverdrup transport has to return somewhere — and it always returns on the western side. The Munk layer width δM = (A/β)^(1/3) sets how thin that western boundary current is. Move the viscosity slider and watch the Gulf Stream get thicker or thinner.

Why it's always western

This is one of the most beautiful results in geophysical fluid dynamics. From Stommel (1948): the boundary current that closes a wind-driven gyre is on the west, not the east, because of β. The asymmetry is built into the planetary vorticity gradient.

Heuristic argument: in the interior, Sverdrup says βv ≈ curl(τ). To close the gyre, the return flow must have opposite vorticity to balance what the interior generates. Friction or viscosity can dissipate vorticity at a boundary. On the western side, the β-term and dissipation work together (both can lose vorticity at the same rate). On the eastern side, they fight, producing an unstable, exponentially growing solution. So the steady answer puts the boundary layer on the west. Always.

Equations

The steady-state, depth-integrated, linear vorticity equation in a closed rectangular basin with no-slip walls:

  β · ∂ψ/∂x − A · ∇⁴ψ + r · ∇²ψ = curl(τ)

This interactive integrates the time-dependent form (with the same right-hand side) until it reaches steady state — that's what you see settling on screen each time you move a slider.

The boundary-layer scales come from balancing terms:
  δ_M = (A/β)^(1/3)   Munk (viscous closure, when A·∇⁴ψ ≈ β·∂ψ/∂x)
  δ_S = r/β   Stommel (frictional closure, when r·∇²ψ ≈ β·∂ψ/∂x)

Whichever is wider wins. In the real ocean both are sub-grid in coarse models — at our 0.35° resolution in the full simulator, neither is properly resolved (see limitations doc).

What to play with

Drag A down. Watch the western boundary current thin and intensify. δM shrinks proportionally to A^(1/3). When δM drops below the grid spacing, the simulation can't resolve it anymore — the readout flags this.

Drag r up. Friction-dominated regime. δS grows linearly in r. With high enough r and low A, you'll see Stommel scaling instead of Munk.

Drag β. Both layer widths shrink as β grows. Stronger planetary vorticity gradient → more concentrated boundary current.

Where this fits

This is what the Sverdrup balance interactive doesn't capture — it solves only the interior. The full amoc-jax simulator (v1a onwards) integrates this same equation on a global grid; the western boundary intensification you see in its output is exactly the same Munk layer, just barely resolved at our resolution.
