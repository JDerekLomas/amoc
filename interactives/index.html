How the ocean simulator works

A guided tour of the physics behind amoc-jax. Each interactive teaches one piece. Start at the top if you've never thought about ocean circulation before; jump anywhere if you know what you're after.

What is this?

The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is a slow river of water inside the ocean. Warm, salty surface water flows north toward Greenland, gives up its heat to the air over Northern Europe, becomes denser, and sinks. The cold, deep water then flows back south. This loop carries roughly a petawatt of heat northward — comparable to the entire global energy consumption — and is the reason Lisbon is warmer than New York at the same latitude.

Climate scientists worry that AMOC could weaken or collapse: melting Greenland adds fresh water at the surface in the north, which makes that water less dense, less likely to sink, and could shut off the loop. Recent observational and modeling work (van Westen, Kliphuis & Dijkstra 2024; Kuhlbrodt & Dijkstra 2025; Castellana et al. 2024) suggests this isn't a hypothetical: the saddle-node bifurcation that causes collapse in toy models has now been demonstrated in state-of-the-art ocean GCMs.

This project is a JAX-based ocean simulator aimed at reproducing that bifurcation in a way you can actually play with. Long-term goal: a SimEarth-style coupled climate sandbox where you turn knobs (CO₂, freshwater forcing, geography) and watch the ocean and atmosphere respond. Browser-native viewer over a real, science-grade physics core.

The interactives below are the building blocks. Each one isolates a single piece of the simulator so you can build intuition before opening the full thing.

The recommended path

First
If you remember nothing else: understand the Stommel two-box AMOC. Two equations, two boxes, a slider for freshwater. The whole tipping-point story compresses into 30 seconds of dragging that slider. This is the conceptual core of the AMOC bifurcation.
Then
How does wind drive the gyres you see on a map? The Stommel box has no spatial structure. The full simulator solves a vorticity equation on a sphere. The Sverdrup balance and Munk boundary layer interactives (coming soon) cover that.
Then
How is the math actually computed? Two key kernels: a Poisson solver (recovers the streamfunction from vorticity) and the Arakawa Jacobian (advects vorticity around without manufacturing energy). Both planned interactives.
Last
Open the full simulator. amoc-jax stitches all the pieces together. The same equations you've been playing with run on observed wind, salt, and temperature fields at 1024×512 resolution to reproduce the real Atlantic and Pacific. Read the design post for the architecture and roadmap.

The pieces, in order of difficulty

Tier 1 — Conceptual

Each one is a small ODE or simple geometry. No grids, no PDEs. Read for 30 seconds, play for two minutes, and you've got the idea.

01
Stommel two-box AMOC start here
Two boxes (warm/salty equator, cold/fresh polar) coupled through density-driven flow. Slowly drag the freshwater slider — watch the AMOC collapse and the hysteresis loop trace itself. Two equations.
In the simulator: this is the conceptual heart of phase v1d (the hosing experiment).
06
Coriolis & β soon
A particle on a rotating sphere. Why moving fluid curves to the right in the north. Why westward-propagating Rossby waves exist.
In the simulator: the β·v term in the vorticity equation.
07
Convective adjustment soon
Cool a column of water from the top — when surface gets denser than what's below, the column overturns instantaneously.
In the simulator: how deep water forms in v1c. The mechanism Stommel's box compresses.
08
Energy balance / ice-albedo soon
1-D Budyko-Sellers with snowball/ice-free bistability. Same kind of saddle-node bifurcation as Stommel, but for the climate as a whole.
A second example of bistability — the AMOC isn't unique in having tipping points.
10
Equation of state soon
T, S sliders → density. Why cold + salty sinks. The linear EOS used in the simulator's buoyancy term.
In the simulator: ρ = ρ₀(1 − αT + βS), used in v1c onwards.

Tier 2 — Spatial

These add a 2D grid. You'll paint or click to set a field, then watch the response. Slightly more setup, much more "feels like the simulator."

02
Sverdrup balance live
Wind curl → meridional transport. The single equation that explains why the subtropical and subpolar gyres exist where they do. Drag β; switch curl pattern; watch the basin-scale ψ field redraw itself.
In the simulator: the steady-state limit of the v1a vorticity equation.
03
Munk boundary layer live
Why the Gulf Stream is on the western side of the Atlantic, not the eastern. Live time-stepped vorticity equation in a closed basin. Slider for viscosity A and β. Watch δ_M = (A/β)^{1/3} shrink — and a red flag when it goes sub-grid.
In the simulator: what sets the western boundary current width in v1a. Currently sub-grid at our 0.35° resolution (see limitations).
09
Rossby waves soon
Drop a disturbance into a rotating fluid; it propagates westward. The mechanism that lets the ocean adjust to wind changes over decades.
In the simulator: emerges from the β term + the Jacobian.

Tier 3 — Numerical kernels

These show the actual computational tricks the simulator uses. Helpful if you want to understand how the model is solved.

04
Poisson playground soon
Paint a vorticity field on a 64×64 grid; the FFT solver inverts to streamfunction in real time. The kernel at the heart of every step.
In the simulator: amoc.poisson — what unblocked the JAX rewrite.
05
Arakawa Jacobian soon
Tracer advection by a velocity field. Show that energy and enstrophy are conserved exactly under the 9-point form — the property that lets us run the simulator for thousands of steps without numerical drift.
In the simulator: amoc.physics.arakawa_jacobian.
