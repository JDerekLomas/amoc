amoc-jax — interactive viewer

amoc-jax (Python/JAX) ran each scenario to steady state offline. Below: a steady-state SST + streamline image, plus the velocity field as a binary. Particles are advected through that velocity field live in your browser — not pre-rendered frames. Adjust the controls to taste; pick a scenario to swap the underlying physics.

The hosing experiment — try it

Click Hosing (weak), then Hosing (strong), then Collapsed AMOC. Watch the AMOC strength readout (lower right) drop as the simulated freshwater flux from melting Greenland builds up in the North Atlantic. This is the Stommel salt-advection bifurcation playing out in a spatially-resolved coupled model — the same mechanism the two-box interactive compresses into 2 ODEs. Recent work (van Westen, Kliphuis & Dijkstra 2024 in Sci Adv; Kuhlbrodt & Dijkstra 2025 in ESD) shows this saddle-node bifurcation is also present in CMIP-class GCMs.

What you're looking at

The colored background is sea-surface temperature from the JAX simulator's steady state — equator warm, poles cold, modulated by basin and mixing. The dark streamlines traced through the field show the time-averaged flow direction (matplotlib streamplot, baked into the image).

The flowing particles are computed live in your browser. Each frame, the velocity field (u, v) is sampled at each particle's position (bilinear interpolation), the particle steps forward, and a trail of fading dots traces its path. When particles drift to land or stagnant water, they respawn in the open ocean. You can crank the count up to 2500 for a thicker pattern.

The velocity field itself is fixed (one steady state per scenario). The particles are just a visualization of that fixed field — they're not running new physics. To see the simulator compute a different state, switch scenarios.

Where this fits

The full simulator is in the amoc-jax/ directory on GitHub. The scenarios shown here came from scripts/render_scenarios.py, which runs the coupled JAX physics for 20,000 steps per scenario then exports the steady state.

For the physics behind what you're seeing, walk through the smaller interactives: Stommel (the AMOC bifurcation in 2 ODEs) → Sverdrup (why gyres exist) → Munk (why Gulf Stream is on the west).
