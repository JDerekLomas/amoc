The model

Cessi (1994) form, two boxes — equatorial (warm, salty) and polar (cold, fresh) — with overturning q driven by their density contrast. In nondimensional units:

  ẋ = (1/τ_T) (1 − x) − x · |x − y|   temperature contrast
  ẏ = F − y · |x − y|   salinity contrast
  q = η (x − y)   overturning strength (positive = thermal mode = surface northward)

The first equation pulls x toward 1 on a timescale τ_T; advection by q couples through |x−y|. The salinity gets only a freshwater flux F as forcing: no relaxation. That asymmetry is what makes the system bistable.

What to play with

Slow ramp F up. Start at F = 0 with q in the thermal mode (positive). Drag F slowly higher. Around F ≈ 1 the thermal branch ends; q flips negative — the salt mode. Now ramp F back down. q stays in the salt mode well past where the thermal branch existed. The hysteresis curve traces out as you go.

The bifurcation point in the upper branch (the "tipping" of AMOC) corresponds to a saddle-node — the stable branch and the unstable branch annihilate. In a real-Earth AMOC, the analog is freshwater input from melting Greenland (Rahmstorf hosing). Recent work (van Westen et al. 2024 in Sci Adv; Kuhlbrodt & Dijkstra 2025 in ESD) shows this saddle-node mechanism is present in CMIP-class GCMs as well as this two-box model.

Where this fits in the simulator

This box model is the conceptual core of v1d in the full amoc-jax simulator. There, instead of two boxes, we'll resolve the same salt-advection feedback on a global lat-lon grid in two layers, prognostic T and S, the same hysteresis under hosing — but it takes thousands of timesteps to play out. Here you can ramp it in seconds.

References: Stommel (1961, Tellus 13:224); Cessi (1994, JPO 24:1911); Rahmstorf (1996, Clim. Dyn. 12:799); van Westen, Kliphuis & Dijkstra (2024, Sci Adv); Kuhlbrodt & Dijkstra et al. (2025, ESD 16:2063).
